Which of the following is a difference between a financial audit and an operational review?
 A. Each gives a different expression of an opinion
 B. Financial audit looks at the present, while an operational review looks at the past
 C. Financial audit reports externally, while operational review internally
 D. An operational review is legally required, while a financial audit is not legally required

You must select an answer.
There is more than one correct answer.

While a financial audit requires an opinion as to the fair presentation of financial statements, the operational review requires no opinion but results in a set of recommendations as to the manner by which to improve operations. The purpose of the financial audit is to report the results of financial operations to interested external third parties. The purpose of the operational review report is to notify internal management of those functional areas needing improvement together with reasonable recommendations as to how to implement such improvements.

The financial audit looks at the past period and the results of operations from a financial viewpoint; the operational review evaluates present conditions in an effort to improve operations in the future. Financial audit reports externally, while operational review internally.

The financial audit is legally required for those organizations coming under the jurisdiction of the SEC and for non-profits and governmental entities political legislation. The operational review is not legally required, but is at the discretion of internal management. The purpose of the financial audit is to report the results of financial operations to interested external third parties. The purpose of the operational review report is to notify internal management of those functional areas needing improvement together with reasonable recommendations as to how to implement such improvements.

The correct answer is C.
