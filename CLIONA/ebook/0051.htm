A general rule is that the subject company represents its “value” in tangible assets—or intangible assets such as patents or copyrights—that can easily be assigned a market, replacement, or liquidation value. It is also important that the individual(s) holding the controlling interest has the ability to liquidate the assets of the organization. This means that if the valuation is being done for a minority interest, an asset-based approach would not be suitable since the minority shareholder would not have the ability to force a sale of the company's assets. An asset-based approach would not be suitable to use for a service company, or one that has few assets.

Revenue Ruling 59-60 states that the asset-based valuation approach may best be applied when the company in question has significant nonoperating assets in the form of investments, such as real estate. Revenue Ruling 59-60 further states the following:

In general, the appraiser will accord primary consideration to earnings when valuing stocks of companies which sell products or services to the public; conversely, in the investment or holding type company, the appraiser may accord the greatest weight to the assets underlying the security to be valued.

Rev. Rul. 59-60, §5(a)
