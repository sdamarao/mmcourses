Net book value

The difference between assets (net of accumulated depreciation, depletion, and amortization) and liabilities as they appear on the balance sheet.

Liquidation value

The net amount that is expected to be received from the sale of an organization's assets and the settlement of its debts. Liquidation can be conducted in either a forced or orderly fashion resulting in different ultimate values.

Investment value

The value that a particular investor places on a business, taking into consideration projected income, projected cash flow, and risk. In other words, the analyst is concerned about the real value of the asset that can be determined only with a higher level of knowledge than the typical investor about the item being valued. For example, the real value of an asset may be different than the price exchanged between a willing buyer and a willing seller since the focus is on a particular investor.

Intrinsic value

The value that is placed on a business, taking into consideration projected income, projected cash flow, and risk as interpreted by an analyst. In other words, the analyst is concerned about the real value of the asset that can be determined only with a higher level of knowledge than held by the typical investor about the item being valued. For example, the real value of an asset may be different than the current market value.

Market value

A type of value, stated as an opinion, that presumes the transfer of property (i.e., a right of ownership or a bundle of such rights), as of a certain date, under specific conditions set forth in the definition of the term identified by the appraiser as applicable in an appraisal. (USPAP 2016-2017 Edition, Definitions, p.3, Lines 92-94)

Going concern value

The value of a business enterprise that is expected to continue to operate into the future. The value created in a company as the result of intangibles, such as an expert workforce or a loyal, established customer base, are part of the going concern value.

Fair market value

The amount for which a business would exchange hands given a willing buyer and a willing seller who are both knowledgeable about the relevant facts of the transaction, are under no compulsion to enter into the transaction, and have no special relationship.
