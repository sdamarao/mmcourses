Per SSVS No. 1:

In performing an engagement to estimate value, the valuation analyst may rely on the work of a third party specialist (for example, a real estate or equipment appraiser). The valuation analyst should note in the assumptions and limiting conditions the level of responsibility, if any, being assumed by the valuation analyst for the work of the third party specialist. At the option of the valuation analyst, the written report of the third-party specialist may be included in the valuation analyst's report.

American Institute of Certified Public Accountants, Statement of Standards for Valuation Services No. 1 , ¶ 20

The adjusted net assets method provides a floor value for valuing a company, since an owner will seldom accept less than the net asset value for a business. An acceptable value could be lower than the fair market value for the entire company, such as replacement value for an insurance claim, but never less than the liquidation value. The owner could liquidate the firm's assets instead of selling the entire business if liquidation would be more profitable. Again, the substitution principle comes into play. Although most sellers hope to receive more than the adjusted net assets value due to the existence of goodwill, the adjusted net assets method can provide a reality check for other valuation methods. No business valuation should establish a value below liquidation value for the assets involved.
