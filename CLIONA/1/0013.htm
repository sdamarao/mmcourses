Since over 90% of businesses in the United States are closely held organizations, a valid determination of the value of such businesses can be important for estate, gift tax, or litigated purposes. What is a business valuation?
 A. The act or process of determining the value of a business enterprise or ownership interest.
 B. The average of the valuations presented by opposing counsel in a litigated case.
 C. The estimation of the net book value using GAAP for a particular valuation date.
 D. The value of a closely-held company calculated by multiplying the number of the firm's outstanding common shares by the most recent selling price.

You must select an answer.
There is more than one correct answer.

Until the Berg vs. Commissioner case in 1991, the courts would often use the average of the business valuations presented by each side to determine value. However, in the Berg case, the court chose the IRS's position since the report provided by its expert was comprehensive, logical, supported, and well presented.

The net book value (cost less depreciation) is generally not used in a business valuation since it will not reflect the standard of value used in a particular valuation engagement such as fair market value, fair value, liquidation value, etc.

Because the stock of closely held organizations seldom changes hands, the most recent selling price is not included in the definition of business valuation.

The correct answer is A.

As defined in the International Glossary of Business Valuation Terms, business valuation is the act or process of determining the value of a business enterprise or ownership interest therein.
