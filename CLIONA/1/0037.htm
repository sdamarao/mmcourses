Lending

Banks are often reluctant to lend funds to closely held businesses. A business valuation can determine the value of assets to be used as collateral. GAAP financial statements reflect the historical, depreciated cost of the business assets, which could be dramatically different than the fair market value of those assets. A valuation may also illustrate how the company can be expected to use those assets to generate future earnings and help convince the lender that enough cash will be generated to repay the potential loan.

example

Look at the following GAAP balance sheet for Dunbar Company:

Current Assets       $   75,000           Total Liabilities       $ 105,000
LT Assets         125,000
           Equity           95,000
Total Assets       $ 200,000
          Total Liab. & Equity       $ 200,000

The local bank is reluctant to lend money to Dunbar for expansion based upon the balance sheet. Using an asset-based approach where the assets are restated using fair market value, it was determined that the long-term assets were actually worth $180,000. This was due to the fact that the plant and equipment had a higher fair market value than the depreciated historical value as stated on the financial statements. Thus, using fair market value, the balance sheet could be restated as:

Current Assets       $   75,000           Total Liabilities       $ 105,000
LT Assets         180,000
           Equity         150,000
Total Assets       $ 255,000
           Total Liab. & Equity       $ 255,000

A banker may be less reluctant to provide an expansion loan for a company with a lower debt to equity ratio based upon the normalized financial statements.
